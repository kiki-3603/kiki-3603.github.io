ESA Research Fellow

Hello there. Ciarán here. I'm an astronomer working at the Space Telescope Science Institute in Baltimore as an ESA Research Fellow. My research focuses on the formation of stars, discs, and planets, and how the external environmental may influence these processes. I have a particular interest in stellar accretion. I observe protoplanetary discs in massive star forming regions with JWST to do this. I am the PI of a Cycle 3 JWST program which targets the proplyds of the ONC with the NIRSpec micro-shutter assembly.

My Research

  • External Irradiation

    How does external UV and X-ray radiation change disc chemistry? How efficiently can these radiation fields destroy discs?. Is star formation itself influenced by external irradiation?

  • Steller Accretion

    A major fascination of mine is stellar accretion. I want to know if the accretion process can be influenced by environmental factors, or if it is driven entirely by internal processes.

  • Late-stage Infall

    Interstellar gas and dust falling back onto protoplanetary discs provides radical new pathways to disrupt the disc, alter its chemistry, and possibly even extend its life.

Publications

Spectral characterisation of the extinction properties of NGC 3603

In this study, we used the Brackett recombination lines from our NIRSpec nebular spectra in order to measure the extinction towards NGC 3603. The measured line ratios deviate from their theoretical value due to extinction. This enabled our determination of the absolute extinction A(V), along with the often more elusive total-to-selective extinction ratio R(V).

Determining stellar accretion rates from Paα and Brβ emission lines.

Using the uninterupted wavelength coverage of NIRSpec, and the efficient multiplexing of the micro-shutter assembly, we measured the stellar accretion rates for a sample of pre-main-sequence stars in NGC 3603. We did this based on their Brγ line luminosities. We then calibrated two strong hydrogen lines for the first time - Paα and Brβ, which are inaccessible from the ground. In the JWST era, Paα and Brβ offer the chance to measure accretion rates for distant, and deeptly embedded young stars.

Kinematic evidence of magnetospheric accretion for Herbig Ae stars.

Our NIRSpec sample of stars in NGC 3603 contained a number of higher mass 'Herbig AeBe' type stars. By exploiting their rich emission line spectra, we performed a kinematic analysis to decipher where these lines originated. Are they produced by magnetospheric accretion flows, or from MHD winds? Surprisingly, our analysis quickly ruled out MHD winds as a possibility, despite their expected importance for Herbig stars. We found that magnetospheric accretion is the most plausible origin for the emission lines. This study sheds light on the accretion mechanism itself for Herbig stars, indicating that they grow in the same way as T Tauri stars - through magnetospheric accretion.

Externally irradiated young stars in NGC 3603

The accretion properties of our sample from NGC 3603 were analysed in detail. We found that despite the harsh environment of NGC 3603, these stars accrete at systematically higher rates compared to similar stars in more quienscent low-mass star forming regions, and they accrete at these high levels for longer - more than 10 million years. We found a tentative link between the accretion rates of our stars and the amount of large scale interstellar gas surrounding them - suggesting that late-stage infall may have a role in explaining these high, and sustained accretion rates.
